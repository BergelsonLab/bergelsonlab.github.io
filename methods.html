Methods & Documentation

We use eye-tracking, EEG, and corpus analysis to examine word learning in infants.


Eye-Tracking

    For typically developing or hearing-impaired infants, we use eye-tracking. We gain insight into infant word learning and word differention using this method.



EEG (electroencephalogram)

    For typically developing children and those with profound hearing or vision impairment, we also use EEG. Using this non-invasive method, we look for certain patterns of brain activity after hearing known words vs similar sounding non-words or unknown words. For the curious: we look for a p100 or N400.



Corpus building and analysis

    We are in the process of building a corpus containing day-long recordings of typically developing infants and infants with profound vision impairment. They simply wear a vest all day with an audio recording device (LENA) nestled inside. We also invite them for a video recorded one hour play session in the lab.

    The Bergelson lab already has a corpus of typically developing infant recordings called SEEDLingS



Audio discrimination tasks

    Words are pronounced differently. One person says a word many different ways many different times. Similarly, different people have their own unique way that they speak overall, even within the same language or dialect (linguists call this an Idiolect). Examining the range of idiolects is what we call examining "talker variability." Also using eye tracking, the Bergelson lab is examining the role of talker variablity in infant language aquisition.

The BLAB is part of Duke's Child and Infant Learning and Development (CHILD) Studies Group.

This research group has looked at infant and child development for many years with the help of parents and children in the Triangle area. Our projects have revealed complex cognitive, social, emotional, and language abilities in babies and children.
